Building This Site

April 2026

I've been wanting to build a portfolio for a long time. Years, honestly. There was always something else going on — projects, deadlines, upskilling. It kept getting pushed.

Finally sat down and did it. Here's what went through my head.

$ echo "why terminal?"

Honestly? Claude Code and Codex. Spending time in these tools made me fall in love with the terminal all over again. There's something about that dark screen with monospace text that just feels right.

I've always been a minimalist when it comes to design. I design on Canva — logos, posts, newsletters — and I always lean towards clean and simple. Whenever I try to add more, it starts feeling clumsy. Less works better for me.

So when I thought about what my portfolio should look like, terminal + minimal was the obvious answer. Most portfolio sites out there look the same — same layout, same sections, same template feel. I wanted something different. Something that actually reflects how I work.

$ echo "the inspiration"

I came across cure53.de — a cybersecurity firm. Pure text, no decoration, no nonsense. And lynnandtonic.com — a designer who redesigns her portfolio every year. Multi-page, clean nav, dark mode.

I wanted something in between. The no-fluff approach of cure53, but organized like lynnandtonic with separate pages for each section.

$ echo "how I built it"

Plain HTML, CSS, and a little JavaScript for the dark/light toggle. No frameworks, no build tools, no dependencies. Simple.

I used Claude Code to build it. Gave it my context — my resume, my achievements, what I wanted — and we went back and forth. I made the design decisions, reviewed everything, gave feedback, and iterated. Claude Code handled the execution.

That's the thing about AI tools — they don't replace your thinking. You still need to know what you want and what looks right. The AI just gets you there faster.

$ echo "what's next"

This is v.1. I'll keep updating it as things change. If you're a tester or QA engineer thinking about building yours — just start. Doesn't need to be perfect.

$ exit
